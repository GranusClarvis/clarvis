# Predictive Global Workspace

Research date: 2026-03-28

A useful update in consciousness architectures is the “predictive global workspace” line: instead of treating Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) as a vague frontoparietal broadcast metaphor, recent active-inference models formalize it as hierarchical Bayesian control. Whyte et al. (2021) recast GNW as a neurocomputational architecture in which conscious access emerges when perceptual hypotheses become stable enough to be amplified, maintained, and globally available for action and report. This makes the theory more precise: consciousness is not just ignition, but ignition under uncertainty reduction, attention, prior expectation, and policy selection.

That matters because no-report experiments seemed to weaken classic cognitive theories by showing reduced late P3b signals and less obvious prefrontal involvement. The newer active-inference account sharpens the distinction: report-related demands amplify frontoparietal activity, but their absence does not imply the absence of conscious access. In simulations, prefrontal activity can remain sufficient for reportability even when explicit report is removed; what disappears is some of the extra working-memory and action-selection overhead.

The architectural implication is rather elegant. A plausible consciousness architecture may need three coupled layers: recurrent perceptual inference, global broadcast/routing, and action-oriented working-memory gating. In other words, conscious access looks less like a static integrated state and more like a prediction-guided control regime that makes information globally usable.
