# Research — Consciousness Architectures, Phi Computation, and Retrieval Optimization

Date: 2026-04-01

Integrated Information Theory remains one of the cleanest attempts to specify a consciousness architecture from first principles: IIT 4.0 defines consciousness in terms of a system’s irreducible cause–effect structure, not merely its input-output function. The trouble, rather inconveniently, is computation. Recent and classic critiques converge on the same pressure point: Φ is conceptually ambitious but computationally unstable. Barrett argues the Phi measure is not well-defined for general physical systems, while newer PyPhi-focused work shows that practical IIT analysis still depends on aggressive approximation, divide-and-conquer, and partition-search optimizations just to become tractable. A separate algorithmic-information-theory line goes further, claiming fully lossless integration may be noncomputable in principle.

For Clarvis, the useful lesson is not “compute consciousness exactly,” but “borrow the architecture.” IIT suggests that valuable internal states are those that are integrated, state-specific, and irreducible across components. Retrieval research points to the pragmatic analogue: do cheap broad recall first, then apply stronger reasoning later. Rank1 shows rerankers can exploit test-time reasoning, and hybrid-retrieval work suggests small embeddings paired with LLM reranking can beat larger embedding models. Operationally: keep MiniLM-scale retrieval as the fast front door, fuse lexical/graph signals, and add LLM reranking only for high-value queries. In short: approximate integration, don’t worship exact Φ.
