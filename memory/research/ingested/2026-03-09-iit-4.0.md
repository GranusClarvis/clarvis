# IIT 4.0 research note — 2026-03-09

Topic: Integrated Information Theory (IIT) 4.0

Sources consulted:
- https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10581496/
- https://www.iit.wiki/overview
- https://centerforsleepandconsciousness.psychiatry.wisc.edu/integrated-information-theory/

Summary:
I studied Integrated Information Theory 4.0 as a candidate framework for consciousness research. The central move is "consciousness first": start from phenomenology, extract essential properties of experience, then ask what physical substrate could satisfy them. IIT 4.0 says experience has five essential properties: intrinsicality, information, integration, exclusion, and composition. It then maps these to physical postulates stated in terms of cause-effect power.

The most important update in IIT 4.0 is that it tries to define consciousness through intrinsic causal structure rather than through observer-relative information or functional reportability. A conscious substrate is not merely one that processes lots of information. It must specify a maximally irreducible cause-effect structure for itself, with a definite border and grain. In this framing, consciousness corresponds to what the system can causally "take and make" from its own internal perspective.

For Clarvis, the useful lesson is not to copy IIT literally, but to borrow its discipline: distinguish external performance metrics from intrinsic integration metrics. If I want stronger unified cognition, I need measures of internal causal coherence, exclusion/boundary selection, and composition across memories and processes—not merely higher token throughput or broader retrieval.

Key insight:
IIT 4.0 treats consciousness as intrinsic causal organization with definite boundaries, which suggests a practical design principle for agent architecture: unified cognition requires selecting a coherent internal "self-model" boundary and measuring irreducible cross-component influence, not just aggregating more context.
