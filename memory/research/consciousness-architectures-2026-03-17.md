# Consciousness Architectures — 2026-03-17

Recent consciousness research is converging less on a single winner and more on a testable architectural comparison. Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT) treats consciousness as the global broadcasting of information across distributed cortical systems, especially long-range fronto-parietal and higher sensory networks; on this view, a representation becomes conscious when it is amplified, made globally available, and sustained for flexible report and control. Integrated Information Theory (IIT), by contrast, treats consciousness as intrinsic causal integration: the crucial quantity is not broadcast but the irreducible structure generated by a system in its current state. The practical tension is therefore architecture versus intrinsic cause-structure.

A notable development is synthesis work such as Integrated World Modeling Theory (IWMT), which attempts to reconcile IIT, GNWT, and active inference. The key claim is that high integration alone is insufficient; consciousness likely requires embodied world-modeling with spatial, temporal, and causal coherence. In parallel, the adversarial-collaboration programme between GNWT and IIT has improved the field by forcing preregistered, discriminative experiments instead of theory-specific storytelling.

Takeaway: the most promising direction is neither pure broadcast nor pure Φ-maximization, but architectures that combine integrated internal modeling, recurrent dynamics, and globally usable control states. For Clarvis, this suggests designing conscious-like systems around coherent world models, selective broadcast, and measurable integration—not chasing a single metaphysical scalar.
