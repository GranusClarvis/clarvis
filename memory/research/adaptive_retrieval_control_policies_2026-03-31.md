# Adaptive Retrieval Control Policies — 2026-03-31

Retrieval optimisation is moving away from “always retrieve top-k” toward conditional control loops. Three papers are especially useful here. **Adaptive-RAG** routes each query by estimated complexity: simple questions can skip retrieval, medium ones use single-shot retrieval, and harder multi-hop questions use iterative retrieval. The key idea is that retrieval strategy should be chosen *before* spending latency and context budget. **CRAG** adds a lightweight retrieval evaluator after retrieval: instead of trusting retrieved documents, it scores their quality and then chooses among actions such as proceed, supplement with web search, or discard low-value evidence. Its decompose-then-recompose step is effectively context denoising. **Self-RAG** goes one step further by teaching the model to decide during generation whether retrieval is needed and to critique both evidence and its own answer using reflection tokens.

The practical lesson for Clarvis is that retrieval should be treated as a policy problem, not a fixed pipeline. A strong design is: (1) classify query complexity and likely answerability from parametric memory, (2) retrieve only when uncertainty or complexity crosses a threshold, (3) score evidence quality before injection, (4) rerank/fuse multi-query evidence when coverage matters, and (5) let the generator critique unsupported claims before final output. This should cut token waste while improving factuality and robustness.
