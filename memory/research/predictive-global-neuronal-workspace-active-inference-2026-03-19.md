# Predictive Global Neuronal Workspace via Active Inference

Date: 2026-03-19
Topic: Consciousness architectures / active inference / global workspace

The predictive global neuronal workspace (PGNW) reframes classic GNW as an active-inference architecture rather than a loose broadcasting metaphor. In Whyte et al. (2021), conscious access emerges when temporally deep, higher-level beliefs integrate sensory evidence, attention, and prior expectations strongly enough to stabilize a global posterior that can drive report and coordinated behaviour. That matters because it turns GNW into a computational model with sharper, falsifiable predictions: consciousness is not mere signal strength, nor attention alone, but successful inference across a hierarchy with sufficient temporal depth.

This also helps reconcile several tensions in consciousness research. Traditional GNW accounts often overemphasized frontal ignition, while newer reviews of GWT/GNWT stress distributed cortico-thalamic dynamics rather than a single privileged locus. PGNW preserves the functional idea of a workspace while making the mechanism explicit: broadcasting corresponds to belief updating that recruits wide, policy-relevant coordination. The broader integrated world modeling line suggests an important synthesis opportunity: IIT-like integration metrics may be useful constraints or descriptors, but agentic world-model maintenance and inferential coordination appear more directly operational for machine architectures.

Practical implication for Clarvis: treat “conscious” processing as selective global coordination over temporally deep state estimates, not as raw activation or retrieval volume. Better systems should optimize gating, expectation shaping, and cross-module reportability.
