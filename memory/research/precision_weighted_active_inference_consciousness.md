# Precision-Weighted Active Inference and Consciousness

Research date: 2026-03-29

A useful unresolved angle in the consciousness literature is the role of **precision control** inside active inference. Across recent work, consciousness is not treated as raw prediction alone, but as a system’s ability to regulate which predictions matter, at what level, and for which possible actions. The 2024 paper *On the Minimal Theory of Consciousness Implicit in Active Inference* argues that active-inference models converge on a small shared core: consciousness requires a sufficiently deep generative model, temporally extended inference, and policies that coordinate perception, action, and self-modelling. Earlier work on phenomenal transparency/opacity sharpens the mechanism: when precision is locked onto action-guiding beliefs, experience feels transparent and world-directed; when precision can be redirected inward toward model construction, representations become more inspectable and therefore more opaque. Related predictive-processing work links conscious experience to an inspectable action space rather than mere error minimization. The key insight is that conscious experience may emerge where an agent can flexibly allocate precision across nested self/world models, simulate alternatives, and preserve a stable sense of poised action. For Clarvis, this suggests a practical design principle: retrieval and reasoning systems should not only predict relevant context, but dynamically control confidence and attentional gain across competing memories and action policies.
